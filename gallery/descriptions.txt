Some of them are just flat, showing how they look immediately after being cut out. Others have been shaped a little bit more, with gentle curves and bends that mimic real hummingbird feathers.<br><br>My primary inspiration comes from feather reference photo libraries where I study how real feathers layer and curve. I'm also deeply inspired by paper artists like <a href="https://www.johanscherft.nl/" target="_blank">Johan Scherft</a> and <a href="https://www.stinebergevensen.nl/" target="_blank">Stine Bergevensen</a>. Their work with paper textures and structural connections has been invaluable to my process.<br><br>This cardboard testing phase is crucial - it allows me to iterate quickly without wasting expensive art paper. Each failed shape teaches me something about where the paper needs to bend, where it needs to stay rigid, and how the feathers should overlap.

1000037106.png| The concept of feather anatomy: each follows different rules. Some have a central shaft, others a thin leading edge; some are for warming, others for flight. This represents the curve of feathers found on the wing and the tail. You can tell the difference between these two because of the shaft: tail feathers have a central shaft for balance, while wing feathers have an off-center shaft to create that thin leading edge needed for flight. So when Im cutting I need to pay attention!! I'm still considering whether or not to overlay a thin strip of paper to show you the shaft but it could be a little bit finicky it's an idea for now...
