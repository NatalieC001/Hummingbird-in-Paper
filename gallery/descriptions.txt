1000037104.jpeg: This photo shows me testing the feather shapes that I've created and printed - sorry, not printed, cut out into cardboard. The feathers are at various stages here. Some of them are just flat, showing how they look immediately after being cut out. Others have been shaped a little bit more, with gentle curves and bends that mimic real hummingbird feathers.<br><br>My primary inspiration comes from feather reference photo libraries where I study how real feathers layer and curve. I'm also deeply inspired by paper artists like <a href="https://www.johanscherft.nl/" target="_blank">Johan Scherft</a> and <a href="https://www.stinebergevensen.nl/" target="_blank">Stine Bergevensen</a>. Their work with paper textures and structural connections has been invaluable to my process.<br><br>This cardboard testing phase is crucial - it allows me to iterate quickly without wasting expensive art paper. Each failed shape teaches me something about where the paper needs to bend, where it needs to stay rigid, and how the feathers should overlap.

1000037106.png: The concept of feather anatomy: each follows different rules. Some have a central shaft, others a thin leading edge; some are for warming, others for flight. This represents the curve of feathers found on the wing and the tail. You can tell the difference between these two because of the shaft: tail feathers have a central shaft for balance, while wing feathers have an off-center shaft to create that thin leading edge needed for flight. So when Im cutting I need to pay attention!!

DIR_3d_modeling: The foundation of the project begins in Blender. Here, the overall shape and structure of the hummingbird are digitally sculpted to form a solid base for the papercraft.
DIR_printing_failures: Translating a digital model to the physical world rarely works perfectly the first time. Early attempts to 3D print the full model revealed limitations, primarily our Bambu A1 Mini's small build plate.
DIR_blender_plugin: Faced with build plate limitations, I created 'JointForge', a custom Blender plugin. This tool automatically separates the large 3D model into smaller component parts with male and female keys, allowing a large armature to be printed on a small plate and easily slotted together.
DIR_paper_cutting: These galleries show the physical testing phase. Notice how different paper weights fold and bend. The physical pieces shown here were cut using the digital paths you can find below in the Templates section.
DIR_templates: Here are the digital vector paths and shapes that are sent to the cutting machine. Compare these paths to the physical cuts in the gallery above!
DIR_embossing_tools: Looking forward: To achieve realistic feather textures, I plan to design and 3D print custom embossing tools in Blender. These will allow me to press the cut paper into shape, mimicking the natural curve of real feathers.
